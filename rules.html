Common referee signals

Memorize the gestures every crew chief uses to communicate with the scorer’s table and the arena.

Traveling

Hands circle around each other to indicate a player moved their pivot foot illegally.

Blocking Foul

Referee places both hands on hips to show the defender did not establish legal guarding position.

Shooting Foul

Arms extended overhead to confirm the offensive player was in the act of shooting.

Flagrant / Unsportsmanlike

Crossed arms over the chest warn the table crew of severe or unnecessary contact.

Rules FAQ

Quick answers to the scenarios fans ask about the most.

How many steps can a player take before it’s traveling?

A player may gather the ball and then take two legal steps before releasing it on a shot or a pass. A third step is whistled as traveling unless a pivot foot remains planted.

What differentiates a charge from a block?

The defender must establish legal guarding position before the offensive player’s gather and cannot be moving laterally into the line of attack. If those boxes aren’t checked, the contact is a blocking foul.

When is a flagrant foul assessed?

Flagrants are judged on the severity of contact, wind-up, follow-through, and whether the play made a legitimate attempt on the ball. Excessive contact becomes Flagrant 1; unnecessary and excessive contact becomes Flagrant 2 with ejection.

What is the restricted area?

The semi-circle four feet from the hoop protects airborne shooters. Defenders cannot draw a charge while standing inside it unless they are the primary defender or the offensive player leads with an extended elbow.
